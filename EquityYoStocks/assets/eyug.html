Introduction

 

This user's guide outlines how to operate the Android app equityYO! in order to make the best use of the software to discover stocks and/or funds that may be suitable to add to your portfolio.

 

Please note that equityYO! plays only a small part in the search for a new stock or fund. Your due-diligence is still required and equityYO! will not be held responsible for your impulsiveness. This means that you must visit the stock's or fund's website and read the appropriate documentation/prospectus before plunking down your cash.

 

Note that not all stocks and funds will be represented within equityYO! due to the limitations of symbols available on the Internet and some symbols may have been left out from these lists. Also, in my navet, I deleted any symbol containing a caret (^) or a dash (-) oopsmy bad This means that both Bershire-Hathaway A (BRK-A) and Bershire-Hathaway B (BRK-B), among others, do not appear in the databasewhich is okay, since I can't afford themand neither can youtee-hee

 

In the interest of full disclosure, the data contained within the on-board SQLite database is not my own and I make no claims to it. Using the R Statistical Software and a lot of frustrating programming constructs (i.e., regular expressions), I was able to screen-scrape data from several websites including, but not limited to, Yahoo! Finance, Google Finance, CEFConnect, and so on. Because I do not own this data, no attempt whatsoever has been made to monetize this app (it's free to download from the Google Play Store) and no attempt will be made so in the future. I was just personally interested in learning Android programming and was, at the same time, in the process of ditching-and-replacing one of my mutual funds. Attempts to find a suitable replacement was difficult with the websites and Android apps as they currently stand prompting me to starting pull data from all over the place in a blind panic. Note that, unless there is an outcry from the equityYO! users, I will not update the data (currently historical data from 2012) contained in the on-board database. If I do decided to update the database, I will have to find a way to purchase the relevant data so that equityYO! is on the up-and-up. If you have a problem with the fact that I screen-scraped the data, please contact me at equityyo@sheepsqueezers.com and I will take the app down from the Google Play Store.

 

Note that you may occasionally see differences in reported prices/NAVs within equityYO! as compared to currently reported prices/NAVs. The on-board database contains historical data from 2012 and stock splits/reverse splits occurring within 2013 may be the cause of these discrepancies.

 

equityYO! was tested on an HTC One S and Barnes & Noble Nook Color Android devices. If you are having trouble with equityYO! on your device, please e-mail me and let me know.

The Main Grid

 

When you start the equityYO! app for the first time, the spash screen will appear (shown below) for a few seconds. This will unpack the compressed database and install it on your device. Note that the database, when compressed, is about 12 megabytes (MB), and uncompressed is about 60 megabytes (MB).

 

 

Once the database has been uncompressed, you will see the main grid which is broken up into the following three major sections:

 

1.    EQUITY STATISTICS this section allows you to start analyzing data for stocks, closed-end funds, mutual (open-end) funds and exchange traded funds (ETFs) as well as view charts for several market indexes (such as the Dow Jones Industrial Average).

2.    EXTERNAL WEBSITES this section allows you to quickly go to a specific website such as Yahoo! Finance, Google Finance, CEF Connect, etc. in order to obtain the latest market data and news.

3.    EQUITYYO! INFORMATION this section allows you to manage your portfolio, change settings, view metadata for the equityYO! 2012 database as well as view this user's guide.

 

 

About Stocks and Funds

 

Before continuing, let's define what we mean by stocks, mutual (open-end/traditional) funds, closed-end funds and exchange traded funds (from Wikipedia and Investopedia).

 

A stock is a type of security that signifies ownership in a corporation and represents a claim on part of the corporation's assets and earnings. There are two main types of stock: common and preferred. Common stock usually entitles the owner to vote at shareholders' meetings and to receive dividends. Preferred stock generally does not have voting rights, but has a higher claim on assets and earnings than the common shares. For example, owners of preferred stock receive dividends before common shareholders and have priority in the event that a company goes bankrupt and is liquidated.

 

You can buy and sell stocks based on its price per share through a stockbroker or electronically through any number of websites such as E*Trade, Fidelity, Vanguard, etc. Note that not all stocks pay out dividends. equityYO! contains dividend-yielding stocks as well as stocks that do not yield any dividends. Stocks can be bought and sold at any time during trading hours.

 

A mutual (open-end/traditional) fund is an investment vehicle made up of a pool of capital collected from many investors (such as you and me) for the purpose of investing in securities such as stocks, bonds, money market instruments and similar assets. Mutual funds are operated by money managers who invest the fund's capital and attempt to produce dividends, capital gains and income for the fund's investors. A mutual fund's portfolio is structured and maintained to match the investment objectives stated in the fund's prospectus. This is why it is so important to thoroughly read the prospectus of any fund you are interested in buying.

 

One of the main advantages of mutual funds is that they give small investors access to professionally managed, diversified portfolios of equities, bonds and other securities, which would be quite difficult (if not impossible) to create with a small amount of capital. Each shareholder participates proportionally in the gain or loss of the fund. Mutual fund units, or shares, are issued and can typically be purchased or redeemed as needed at the fund's current net asset value (NAV) per share. Note that some mutual funds yield dividends as well as capital gains from their underlying securities. This is above and beyond any gains/losses due to the increase/decrease in the net asset value (NAV) of the fund itself. Mutual funds can be purchased in one of several ways depending on the mutual fund company itself (via mail, online, etc.). You can sell a mutual fund at any time during the course of the trading day, but the actual sales transaction does not occur until the end of the trading day. If you contact your mutual fund company after normal trading hours and ask them to sell your shares in a mutual fund, those shares will be sold at the end of the next trading day.

 

A closed-end fund is similar to a mutual (open-end/traditional) fund except that a closed-end fund contains a fixed amount of the underlying securities when the fund is first established. This differs from a mutual (open-end/traditional) fund which purchases additional shares of the underlying securities when an investor (such as you and me) purchases shares in the mutual (open-end/traditional) fund. Since there are a fixed number of shares available, the ability to purchase shares in a closed-end fund is based on the availability of outstanding shares. Unlike mutual (open-end/traditional) funds, and more similar to stocks, you can buy and sell shares in a closed-end fund during normal trading hours and the transaction will occur at or near the time the request is made. This differs from a mutual (open-end/traditional) fund which sells shares at the end of the trading day. Be aware that trading volume for some closed-end funds may be small and this may hamper your attempts to buy or sell shares. (But, please don't let that comment put you off adding a closed-end fund to your portfolio.)

 

Unlike both stocks and mutual (open-end/traditional) funds, closed-end funds have both a net asset value (NAV) per share as well as a price per share based on the market's valuation of the fund. This strangeness is called the closed-end fund puzzle. If the fund's price per share is higher than its NAV, the fund is said to be trading at a premium; otherwise, it is trading at a discount. It may not make sense to you that a closed-end fund can sell at a price per share higher or lower than its NAVyou're not aloneit doesn't seem to make sense to a lot of financial analysts as well!

 

Note that a brand new closed-end fund has an annoying tendency for its price per share to take a nose-dive right out of the starting gate; so, be cautious when thinking about purchasing shares in a brand new closed-end fund.

 

An exchange traded fund (ETF) is similar to stocks (in that you can buy and sell them during the course of the trading day) and closed-end funds (in that they hold a basket of underlying securities and are associated with a net asset value (NAV)). ETFs may sound very similar to closed-end funds except that any potential discount or premium due to the difference between the NAV and the price per share is smaller than for closed-end funds and is (supposedly) temporary.

 

EQUITY STATISTICS

 

In the main grid section labeled EQUITY STATISTICS, there are five touchable buttons that allow you to analyze stocks, closed-end funds, mutual (open-end/traditional) funds, exchange traded funds and market indexes. Since the equityYO! stocks, closed-end funds, mutual funds and exchange traded funds options are very similar in functionality, we will just show the functionality available in the Stocks option. Note, though, that some options display slightly different information than other options.

 

When you touch the Stocks option, you will be brought to the SYMBOLS list tab which will be blank (as shown below left).

 

 

On the right side of the image, you will see a small arrow pointing to the left. If you click that arrow, a sliding drawer will appear (see below) which allows you to subset and sort the data related to stocks.

 

 

At the top of the sliding drawer, you can select five different subsetting options:

1.    Stats (Short) this option contains a short list of subsetting options (for those of you who don't really care about EPS, PE Ratio, etc.).

2.    Stats (Long) this option, the default, contains the full list of subsetting and sorting options.

3.    Lookup (Symbol) this option allows you to subset the data based on a single stock symbol (such as MSFT for Microsoft or GOOG for Google).

4.    Search (Name) this option allows you to subset the data based on your search criteria within the name. For example, you can search for biotech in the name and any company name containing biotech will be displayed.

5.    Search (Summary) this option allows you to subset the data based on search criteria within the full company summary. For example, you can search for fund of funds in the summary and any company summary containing fund of funds will be displayed.

 

Now, at the bottom of the sliding drawer there are two buttons (as shown below):

1.    Submit this will submit your subsetting and sorting criteria to the database and, if your criteria yields results, a list of the appropriate symbols will be displayed. Note that the sliding drawer will automatically close if your subsetting criteria yielded one or more symbols, but it will not close if it did not yield any symbols.

2.    Reset this button will reset the form.

 

 

Note that many of the drop-down boxes contain subsetting criteria based on quartiles.

 

 

As you can see above, the SYMBOLS tab has been populated with a list of symbols that have met our criteria. If you would like to save a symbol to your Portfolio, long-tap the entry and it will be added to your Portfolio. We talk more about the Portfolio later on. Note that the data is automatically sorted ascending by symbol if you do not change the sort order. To change the sort order, click the small circle to the left of the drop-down box to display an up-arrow image, indicating that you want the data sorted by ascending order, or a down-arrow image, indicating that you want the data sorted by descending order. As shown below on the left, the user is requesting the Computed Yield be sorted in ascending order whereas the image on the right indicates that the user wants a decending sort. To remove the sort completely, click on the image until you get back the image containing a dash.

Explanation of Statistics

 

In this section, we explain the fields that appear throughout equityYO!. Note that some of the fields listed below do not appear in the sliding drawer, but only appear within the database itself.

 

1.    Type indicates the type of data (C=Closed-End Funds, E=Exchange Traded Funds, O=Mutual Funds and S=Stocks).

2.    Symbol the symbol related to the company or fund. For example, MSFT is the symbol for stock Microsoft and VWENX is the symbol for the mutual fund Vanguard Wellington Fund.

3.    sName the name of the company or fund. For example, Microsoft or Vanguard Wellington Fund.

4.    sInfo the full company or fund summary as it appears on Yahoo! Finance or other sites.

5.    sSiteURL the web address (URL) of the company or fund, if available.

6.    sPV contains the Yahoo! Finance "People viewing also viewed" data, if available. This data is used to generate the ALSO VIEWED INFORMATION graph.

7.    IsDJIA indicates whether the stock is listed on the Dow Jones Industrial Average and is used in the Index Inclusion drop-down box.

8.    IsSP500 indicates whether the stock is listed on the S&P 500 and is used in the Index Inclusion drop-down box.

9.    IsNAS100 indicate whether the stock is listed on the NASDAQ 100 and is used in the Index Inclusion drop-down box.

10.  IsRUS2000 indicates whether the stock is listed on the RUSSEL 2000 and is used in the Index Inclusion drop-down box.

11.  ComputedDividend this is an equityYO! computed statistic which is the sum of the dividends and capital gains (despite the name of the field) for 2012. This may be slightly different from DisplayedDividend (see below).

12.  ComputedYield this is an equityYO! computed statistic which is the yearly yield based on the ComputedDividend. This may be slightly different from the DisplayedYield (see below).

13.  DividendFrequency the number of dividends/capital gains paid out in 2012. Note that, despite the name of the field, this count contains capital gains as well. Note that due to the Fiscal Cliff, many stocks/funds may have paid out additional dividends/capital gains at the end of 2012 than is usual.

14.  DividendConsistency this is an equityYO! computed statistic which indicates how consistent the dividends/capital gains values are during the course of the year. An "A" indicates that the dividends/capital gains are very consistent (only very slight deviations) during the year. An "E" indicates that the dividends/capital gains are not very consistent (large deviations) during the year. Unfortunately, those stocks/funds that paided out a single dividend/capital gain during 2012 is marked as an "A", which will be corrected in a future release.

15.  PriceSlopeSign this is an equityYO! computed statistic which uses Linear Regression to determine the slope of the Price data across the year. This value is coded as either increasing or decreasing. An increasing PriceSlopeSign indicates that the series of Price data for the year shows an overall increase, despite the fact that the ending price/NAV for the year may be lower than the beginning price/NAV for the year.

16.  sNetAssetValue/NetAssetValue this is the Net Asset Value, when available, that appears on Yahoo! Finance's website. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

17.  sMktCapNetAssets/MktCapNetAssets this is either the Market Capitalization or the Net Assets (when available) based on the Type. For stocks, it is the Market Capitalization; otherwise, it is the Net Assets. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

18.  sTargetEstimate/TargetEstimate this is the target estimate for the stock or fund, when available. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

19.  sBeta/Beta this is the Beta statistic, when available. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

20.  sPERatio/PERatio this is the price/earnings ratio, when available. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

21.  sEPS/EPS this is the earnings per share, when available. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

22.  sAverage3MonthVolume/Average3MonthVolume this is an average of the volume across three months. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

23.  sDisplayedDividend/DisplayedDividend this is the displayed dividend appearing on Yahoo! Finance's website. Note that this field should contain just the sum of the dividends for the year (and no capital gains). The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

24.  sDisplayedYield/DisplayedYield this is the displayed yield appearing on Yahoo! Finance's website. Note that this field should have been computed off of the DisplayedDividend. The first field is the exact character string whereas the second field is the numeric translation of it.

25.  sMorningstarRating/MorningstarRating this is the five-point Morningstar Rating for a fund, if available. The text field contains "1 stars" "5 stars" whereas the numeric field contains a 1 5.

26.  PremDisc this is an equityYO! computed statistic and is the premium or discount of either a closed-end fund or exchange traded fund based on the available NAV and price on the last trading day of 2012.

27.  MinMonthPriceNAV this is the price/NAV appearing on the first available month of 2012. Note that some stocks/funds may not have begun trading until later in the year.

28.  MaxMonthPriceNAV this is the price/NAV appearing on the last available month of 2012. Note that some stocks/funds may have ended trading before the end of the year.

29.  ComputedGrowth this is an equityYO! statistic and is the estimated computed growth, as a percent, based on the MinMonthPriceNAV and MaxMonthPriceNAV (100*(MaxMonthPriceNAV - MinMonthPriceNAV)/MinMonthPriceNAV).

30.  FundFamily this is the name of the company that runs the fund (such as Vanguard, Eaton-Vance, etc.)

31.  Sector this is the name of the sector that the stock or closed-end fund is in (such as Basic Materials, Finance, Consumer Goods, etc.).

32.  Industry this is the name of the industry that the stock or closed-end fund is in (such as Gold, Aluminum, Silver, etc.) and is based on the sector that the stock or closed-end fund is in.

33.  Category this is name of the category that the exchange traded fund or mutual (open-end/traditional) fund is in (such as Aggressive Allocation, Health, Utilities, etc.).

34.  Month this is the number of the month within 2012 (1=January, 12=December).
